## 📋 Research Overview

India's FMCG packaging sector operates at the intersection of three critical challenges:

1. **Regulatory pressure** — Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) mandates under the Plastic Waste Management Rules require brands to take back and recycle packaging at scale
2. **Informal sector dependence** — 95% of India's waste collection and sorting is performed by the informal sector (waste pickers, kabadiwalas, aggregators)
3. **Technology opportunity** — AI capabilities in computer vision, optimisation, and traceability can dramatically accelerate circular material flows

This dissertation investigates **how AI can accelerate the circular economy transition** while navigating the governance tensions inherent in India's dual-structure packaging value chain.

---

## 🔬 Research Questions

1. **How can AI capabilities be systematically mapped** across the FMCG packaging lifecycle to identify intervention points for circular acceleration?

2. **What governance tensions emerge** when AI-enabled optimisation interacts with informal-sector livelihoods, data sovereignty, and EPR compliance?

3. **What configurations of AI-enabled circular practices** are viable within India's regulatory and market context?

### Four Configurations Identified

| Configuration | AI Focus | Circular Outcome | Governance Tension |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Design-Led** | Simulation & material optimisation | Design-for-recyclability | Limited — upstream intervention |
| **Collection-Led** | Route optimisation & demand prediction | Improved collection efficiency | Informal sector displacement risk |
| **Sorting-Led** | Computer vision & robotic sorting | Higher-purity recycled material | Capital vs. labour substitution |
| **Traceability-Led** | Blockchain + IoT material tracking | EPR compliance & transparency | Data sovereignty, cost burden on SMEs |

## 🌍 Key Findings

### 1. The Informal Sector Paradox

India's waste management system depends critically on its informal sector — an estimated **4 million waste pickers** collect, sort, and aggregate packaging waste. AI-enabled sorting and collection systems risk displacing these livelihoods unless explicitly designed for integration rather than substitution.

### 2. Compliance vs. Transformation Trade-off

EPR compliance creates a *minimum bar* for corporate action but does not incentivise genuine circular innovation. AI traceability systems optimised purely for compliance reporting may miss opportunities for material-flow optimisation that deliver both environmental and economic value.

### 3. Data Sovereignty in Emerging Markets

AI-enabled circular systems generate valuable material-flow data. The question of who owns, controls, and benefits from this data — brands, waste aggregators, municipalities, or platform intermediaries — remains unresolved and politically contested.

### 4. The Design-Stage Leverage Point

The highest-impact intervention point is **packaging design**, where AI simulation can optimise for recyclability before production. This upstream approach avoids the governance tensions associated with downstream collection and sorting automation.

## 🏛️ Implications

### For Managers

- Prioritise **design-stage AI interventions** — highest circular impact, lowest governance risk
- Build **informal sector integration** into technology adoption roadmaps from day one
- Use EPR compliance as a *floor*, not a ceiling — circular innovation delivers competitive advantage beyond regulatory minimums

### For Policymakers

- Develop **inclusive technology adoption guidelines** that protect informal-sector livelihoods
- Establish **data governance frameworks** for material-flow platforms before corporate dependencies lock in
- Create **financial incentives** for genuine circular innovation beyond compliance documentation

### For Future Research

- Quantitative assessment of AI-enabled circular systems in Indian FMCG pilot deployments
- Longitudinal study of informal sector adaptation to technology transitions
- Comparative analysis across emerging market regulatory contexts (India, Brazil, Indonesia)
